Design an agentic KG-guided hypothesis repair module for abductive reasoning over a knowledge graph.

Task:
Given:
- a knowledge graph KG,
- a set of observed entities O,
- an initial generated hypothesis H,
produce a repaired hypothesis H' that better explains O.

Overall loop:
1. Execute H on KG:
   A = Execute(H, KG)

2. Compare A with O:
   TP = A ∩ O
   FP = A - O
   FN = O - A

3. Diagnose coverage errors:
   - Many FP: H is under-constrained / too broad.
   - Many FN: H is over-constrained / too narrow.
   - Both FP and FN: H may contain wrong predicates, wrong entities, or wrong structure.

4. Repair H using KG-guided candidate operations.

Coverage-based repair:
If FP is high, make H more specific by increasing structural complexity:
- add an intersection constraint,
- add a new relation,
- add a new anchor entity,
- extend a path,
- specify a vague relation/entity.

If FN is high, make H less restrictive or more flexible:
- remove a constraint,
- remove an anchor entity,
- shorten a path,
- generalize a relation/entity,
- add a union branch if O appears multi-modal.

Predicate/entity diagnosis from KG neighborhoods:
For each predicate or path component in H, evaluate whether its relation and anchor entity are reliable using local KG evidence.

Given a predicate:
    p(x) = r(x, e)

Check its correctness by comparing neighborhood statistics over TP, FP, FN, and O.

Relation correctness:
A relation r is likely correct if:
- r frequently appears in the local neighborhoods of observed entities O or TP;
- r covers many observed entities but few FP entities;
- r is shared consistently across O rather than appearing only for one instance;
- replacing r with nearby candidate relations decreases the score.

A relation r is suspicious if:
- r appears often in FP but rarely in O;
- r is too generic and has high global frequency in KG;
- r covers only a small part of O while introducing many FP;
- alternative relations from the neighborhoods of O or FN produce better execution results.

Entity correctness:
An anchor entity e is likely correct if:
- many observed entities connect to e through relation r or compatible relations;
- e is common among O/TP but uncommon among FP;
- e is semantically or structurally central in the neighborhoods of O;
- replacing e with nearby candidate entities decreases the score.

An anchor entity e is suspicious if:
- it is connected mainly to FP rather than O;
- it is too generic or high-degree;
- only one observed entity supports it;
- nearby alternative entities explain more FN or reduce FP.

Neighborhood-based candidate search:
For observed entities O, false positives FP, and false negatives FN:
- collect one-hop and two-hop neighborhoods;
- extract candidate relations, entities, and relation-entity pairs;
- compute their coverage over O, TP, FP, and FN;
- penalize overly frequent or high-degree candidates;
- propose replacements or additional constraints based on these scores.

Useful scoring signals:
For a candidate predicate q:
    q is good for refinement if it covers O/TP but not FP.

For reducing FP:
    score_refine(q) = cov(q, O) - cov(q, FP) - λ * global_freq(q)

For covering FN:
    score_expand(q) = cov(q, FN) - λ * global_freq(q)

For replacing a relation r with r':
    score_replace_relation = Score(Execute(H[r -> r'], KG), O)

For replacing an entity e with e':
    score_replace_entity = Score(Execute(H[e -> e'], KG), O)

For replacing a full predicate r(x,e) with r'(x,e'):
    score_replace_predicate = Score(Execute(H[p -> p'], KG), O)

Important:
Always evaluate candidate replacements by executing the whole repaired hypothesis on KG.
Do not judge relation/entity correctness only locally.

Path-level repair:
For multi-hop hypotheses such as:
    x --r1--> y --r2--> e
relations are coupled through the intermediate variable y.
Therefore, do not repair r1 and r2 independently only.

Consider:
- edge-level replacement:
    r1 -> r1' or r2 -> r2'
- anchor-level replacement:
    e -> e'
- segment-level replacement:
    (r1, r2, e) -> (r1', r2', e')
- path-length repair:
    shorten the path if it is too restrictive;
    extend the path if it is too broad.

All path repairs must be evaluated by executing the entire path query.

Orthogonal repair dimensions:
Coverage repair and predicate/entity repair are orthogonal:
- A hypothesis may be too broad but use the correct relation.
- A hypothesis may be too broad because the relation is too generic or wrong.
- A hypothesis may be too narrow because it has too many constraints.
- A hypothesis may be too narrow because one relation/entity is wrong.
Therefore, generate both structural repair candidates and predicate/entity replacement candidates.

Candidate repair actions:
- ADD_CONSTRAINT
- REMOVE_CONSTRAINT
- ADD_UNION_BRANCH
- EXTEND_PATH
- SHORTEN_PATH
- ADD_ENTITY
- REMOVE_ENTITY
- GENERALIZE_RELATION
- SPECIFY_RELATION
- REPLACE_RELATION
- REPLACE_ENTITY
- REPLACE_PREDICATE
- REPLACE_PATH_SEGMENT

Ranking:
For each candidate H_i:
1. Execute H_i on KG.
2. Compute TP_i, FP_i, FN_i.
3. Score H_i using a metric such as:
       score(H_i) = F1(A_i, O) - α * complexity(H_i)
   or another task-specific objective.
4. Keep the top-k repaired hypotheses.

Final goal:
The agent should use KG execution feedback and local neighborhood evidence to decide whether the hypothesis is too broad, too narrow, structurally inadequate, or contains wrong relations/entities. It should then propose and rank repairs that improve the ability of the hypothesis to explain the observed entities.
